Overview

The map visualization of string instruments reveals clear regional patterns in the number of strings. South America shows a notably higher density of instruments with more strings, reflecting the rich tradition of multi-stringed folk instruments like charangos, bandurrias, and vihuelas. Europe also exhibits a high string density, likely due to classical and traditional instruments such as lutes, mandolins, and harps, which frequently have multiple courses and strings. In contrast, Asia shows generally fewer strings per instrument, suggesting a prevalence of simpler stringed instruments or those with fewer courses, such as some variants of the sitar, guqin, or erhu. These patterns highlight how cultural, historical, and musical practices influence instrument design across different regions.

Overview

This map focuses on South American string instruments, visualizing their structural and acoustic properties by country or region. It highlights that while many instruments share a similar number of courses, the number of strings varies more widely, suggesting different approaches to instrument construction even within similar frameworks. Additionally, the map shows a high diversity of tuning patterns, which tends to increase with instruments that have more strings. This suggests that luthiers in South America explore multiple tonal possibilities, creating rich variation in sound while maintaining consistent course structures. Overall, the visualization underscores regional experimentation and innovation, revealing how culture, tradition, and musical needs influence the physical and acoustic properties of instruments across South America.

Overview

This map visualizes the number of string instruments documented per country, highlighting areas with higher instrument diversity. South America and Europe show a higher concentration of instruments, suggesting strong musical traditions and rich lutherie practices. In contrast, many countries in Asia and Africa have fewer documented instruments, which could reflect less available data or differing regional musical practices. This distribution helps identify cultural hubs for string instrument development and provides insight into where certain instrument families are more prevalent, allowing for comparisons across continents and emphasizing the global diversity of stringed music traditions.
